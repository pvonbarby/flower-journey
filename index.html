"How did I
get there?"

Nowadays, it's easy to fill our gardens with floweres from around the world and some flowers, such as the tulip, seem incredibly mundane. Tracing the journey of today's most common flowers tells the story of the imperial botany project of the 15th and 16th centuries and reveals how far these flowers have traveled from their native climates.

The Tulips' Journey

The tulip is native to the Tien Shan mountain region. Records show it was cultivated in Istanbul as early as 1055. In 1554, Ogier Ghiselih de Busbecq, the ambassador to the sultan of Turkey, sent seeds to Vienna, where Carlos Clusius headed the botanical garden. Clusius took the seeds to Leiden in the Netherlands, where he laid out the physic garden at the city's new university. Entrepreneurs began establishing nurseries around the Netherlands at the start of the 17th century and soon tulip collecting took off. Tulips were exported to Berlin, Paris, and the Kew gardens in London. Within a year, the price of tulips more than doubled and speculators took advantage of the market. By the 1620s, single-colored varieties were selling for 12 florins a pound, while the average annal income was about 150 florins. By 1637, sellers began to outnumber buyers and the market collapsed. The Netherlands remains one of the largest exporters of bulbs today. The tulip came to the United States around 1698, as Dutch emigrants moved to the US.
